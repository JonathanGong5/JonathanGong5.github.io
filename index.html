Did States With A Stricter Governmental Response to COVID-19 Experience Worse Economic Outcomes?¶

Jonathan Gong, CMSC320 Final Project, May 16th, 2022¶

1. Introduction¶

Since the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, many U.S. citizens have expressed concerns about the economic repercussions resulting from the governments policy responses. These reservations combined with inherent disagreements about the overall health risk imposed by the virus has led different state and local administrations to impose varying levels of COVID-19 restrictions in terms of both breadth and stringency. For instance, whereas Democrat-led states in the northeast tended to pass more rigorous containment policies, Republican-led states in the midwest were generally more lenient in their implementation of government measures.

However, the enforcement of COVID-19 policies was hardly uniform amongst regions. Given the United States federalist nature, both President Trump and President Bidens administrations heavily relied on a decentralized approach towards combating the virus. Indeed, although federal restrictions and guidelines certainly played a role in influencing everyday Americans lives, the vast majority of decision-making regarding COVID-19 was left to individual state governments.

The decentralized nature of the U.S. approach resulted in a wide array of unique responses to the pandemic. The purpose of this project is to leverage this variability by examining whether states with stricter governmental responses experienced worse economic outcomes. Under the hypothesis that greater stringency translates to worse economic outcomes, I seek to identify which specific policies incurred the greatest cost and by how much. It is also plausible that reducing the spread of COVID-19 could have resulted in positive economic externalities in the long-run by fostering a quicker return to normalcy.

To accomplish this, I used monthly state-level employment and earnings measurements as well as ordinal indices from Oxfords COVID-19 government response tracker to perform an entity and time fixed-effects regression. I found that the overall effect of higher stringency on economic outcomes was negative but negligible. Specifically, the policies most associated with worse economic conditions were stricter workplace closure mandates and stay-at-home requirements, but neither had a profound scalable impact.

2. Data Collection for the Independent Variables¶

a) The Oxford COVID-19 Government Response Tracker¶

This data measures the stringency of each COVID-19 policy response for each state in the U.S. over time (January 2020 to March 2021). It was created by researchers at the Blavatnik School of Government as part of their ongoing endeavor to create the Oxford COVID-19 Government Response Tracker (an aggregatation of information regarding each government's response-level to the pandemic across the entire world). The data I am using is from their US-specific subnational dataset. I downloaded this data directly as an Excel file from their website. Each sheet in the excel file corresponds to a different policy measure.

To measure the stringency of any given COVID-19 response policies, the researchers have designed their own system of ordinal metrics. As an example, the variable for Workplace Closing is encoded as follows:

0 - no measures
1 - recommend closing (or recommend work from home) or all businesses open with alterations resulting in significant differences compared to non-COVID-19 operation.
2 - require closing (or work from home) for some sectors or categories of workers
3 - require closing (or work from home) for all-but-essential workplaces (e.g. grocery stores, doctors) (Variation in US states COVID-19 policy responses).

An explanation of how each policy measure is encoded can be found in this codebook. For the purposes of this assignment, I hand-selected five specific COVID-19 policies to determine their influence on state-level economic outcomes. They are: (1) Workplace Closures, (2) Restrictions on Gathering, (3) Closing Public Transport, (4) Stay-At-Home Requirements (5) Facial Coverings/Mask Mandates. I also extracted the overall composite index for governmental stringency for visualization purposes.

b) Number of new COVID Cases as a Controlling Variable¶

One of the benefits of fixed-effects regression is that it can control for omitted variables in panel data when the omitted variables vary across entitites but do not change over time. Therefore, if there is a variable that varies by entity and time that it is wise to control for it. One such variable for this regression analysis is the number of new COVID cases in each month. As such, I will include this data as a controlling regressor. I obtained this data from the [New York Times COVID Data Tracker] (https://github.com/nytimes/covid-19-data/blob/master/us-states.csv).

  1. There appears to be a stark contrast between observations that can be characteried as "low stringency" and observations that can be characterized as "high stringency." In other words, it is not common for a state to have exhibited a medium (20-40) level of stringency at any given time. This begs the question: is this natural disparity due to the tendency that of some states to never reach a high level of stringency? OR did all states exhibit a jump in their stringency level (presumably after shutdowns occured)?

  2. The overall correlation between stringency and employment seems to be fairly weak but negative. As the index for stringency increases, the employment-population ratio trends downwards but there are high residuals across the board. By contrast, the relationship is slightly upwards trending for average weekly earnings but the correlation remains fairly weak.

The only observable difference between the population and GDP standardization methods is that the GDP standardization method appears to eliminate the occurence of one extreme outlier around the 50 ticker for the x-axis. This indicates that the GDP standardization method may be better for regression analysis

5. Preparing for the Regression Analysis¶

In order to conduct an entity and fixed effects regression, I need to create binary variables corresponding to each monthYear and each state. Importantly, I must not create a binary variables for each entity/time observation as this would lead to perfect multicollinearity. As such, I will create 49 entity-specific binary variables and 14 time-specific variables.
